# Cold-email rules

Rachel Konrad's 10 rules + Heidi Roizen's mailing rules + Danny Hertzberg's six elements. Everything you need to write a cold email someone actually answers.

## Philosophy

A cold email is a **pitch**. Not a request. Not an introduction. Not a resume summary.

**JFK principle:** Don't pitch yourself. Pitch what you can do for them. *"Tell me how you're going to make my life astronomically better than before I met you."*

You have **15 seconds**. The recipient gets dozens of these a week. Everything below exists to survive those 15 seconds.

## The 10 rules

### 1. Know something about them and exploit it
LinkedIn, podcasts, blog posts, recent news, portfolio if VC. People drop personal details in 20-minute podcasts — use them. Google News search is non-negotiable.

### 2. Begin with what they do not know
Don't open with flattery — they know what they've done. **"Tell me a secret about the future."** One sentence with an insight about where their industry is going that they haven't heard. Slightly wrong is fine. Generic is fatal.

### 3. Subject line that cannot be ignored
Personal, timely, unusual. **Not** "Thank you," "Hoping to connect," or "Quick question."

The pattern in subject lines that worked in class:
- A specific shared object or moment with the recipient (a gift, a year, a place you both know)
- Your most distinctive credential, especially if incongruous with the role you're applying to (the athlete moving into law, the immigrant moving into tech)
- A small, named relationship to a shared place or institution — but only if the place is small enough to mean something specific

### 4. Find a mutual contact and name them
"X suggested I reach out." The bar is higher than a LinkedIn connection — must be someone you've actually engaged with.

**Heidi's rule for forwarding:** if someone agrees to forward your email, send three individually tailored versions so all they have to do is hit forward. Don't make them rewrite.

### 5. "Like you" — compare yourself, specifically
Genuine and specific.

✅ A specific shared sensory detail from your shared origin or experience — the food you grew up eating, the neighborhood you walked, the year you immigrated, the specific job you both did before the one you're emailing about.
❌ *"Like you, I believe AI will transform the future."* (meaningless)
❌ *"Like you, but at a vastly smaller scale."* (self-diminishing — they already know that)

### 6. Tell a story that makes them feel something
A **scene**, not a summary. Multisensory. A date, a place, a line of dialogue, an image.

The pattern that works in class examples: a specific year, a named place, one concrete action, optionally one line of dialogue or one sensory detail. The classic move is *inverted hierarchy* — lead with humility about your short tenure or small scale, then make that the reason the recipient should care. *"You have N years at this. I have three weeks. And that is why I'm reaching out."* The contrast disarms; the curiosity carries the email.

### 7. Pick a lane
Do not cover everything on your resume. **One** example that shows how you can help **this specific person**. Link LinkedIn in the signature for the rest.

For consultants: talk about projects you took from concept through execution. *"This is what it looked like on launch day, and I did it."*

### 8. Confident but humble
Adam Bryant's *"desirable confidence."* If in doubt, have a friend read it first.

### 9. Short with a small, easy ask
Under 200 words.

❌ *"Coffee for an hour to give me career advice."*
✅ *"Ten minutes because I'm choosing between a PM role at Google and director of products at a 20-person startup. I've fleshed out my pros and cons. I'd love your take."*

Read from the recipient's eyes. Eliminate every step you've created for them that you could do yourself.

Leave the door open for no: *"I know that's a big ask, so no is a perfectly fine answer."*

Never say *"if you ever find yourself near Stanford."* Say *"I will come to you, anywhere, anytime."*
Never suggest *"coffee."* Suggest something specific to them — a ride if they cycle, a swim if they swim.

### 10. Offer something in return
The single best way to **quintuple** response rates from CEOs is to stop asking and start offering.

Invite them to speak at a class, a podcast, an event. CEOs manage 12–18 months out — a lunch request gets ignored, an invitation to keynote at GSB gets answered. Then you moderate, meet them in the green room, and have done something genuinely valuable.

## Heidi Roizen's mailing rules

1. Make your ask **as small as possible**.
2. Make the ask **as easy to act on** as possible.
3. **Leave the door open for no.**
4. **Don't make people copy or forward awkwardly** — send tailored versions.
5. **Be confident but humble.**
6. **Have a friend read it** before sending.
7. **Don't follow up with "just following up."** Write a new email with new information.

## Danny Hertzberg's six elements (Base10, ex-Sequoia)

1. **Do your homework** — research shows you care; specific industry insights are impossible to ignore
2. **Add value** — gentle ego stroke; share deal flow or insights that help their work
3. **Assume human, not robot** — AI-generated emails are increasingly detectable
4. **Find and empower a wing person** — don't brag directly; have someone introduce you with your accomplishments
5. **Embrace rejection** — 15–20% response rate is normal
6. **Make yourself memorable** — handwritten follow-up notes can't be written by AI

## The follow-up rule

If your first email is read but not answered, **do NOT** reply *"just following up."* Write a new email that references the first without being needy. Maintain or escalate the ask. New information, new hook, new reason for urgency.

## The AB test

The exact same email sent to `jobs@anthropic.com` got **zero** response. Sent directly to the head of executive recruiting at Anthropic, replied at 8:25 AM the next morning.

**Find the actual person.** Guess the email format. BCC variations. Use Rocket Reach. Never `jobs@`, `pitches@`, or `careers@`.

## Length: under 200 words. Always.

If your draft is over 200, you have not picked a lane. Cut.
