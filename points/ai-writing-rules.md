# AI writing rules

How to use AI without sounding like AI. From Kramon's class, the *Claude Cowork* workflow doc, and the centaur-writer essay.

## The core problem

> *"You want to be one in a million. AI by definition makes you one of a million."* — Kramon

AI defaults to the median. Median writing is forgettable. Your job is to push every draft above the median.

## The centaur model

You + AI, not AI alone. Hands stay on the keyboard. AI is a sparring partner, not a ghostwriter.

Anthropic's framing: chat is a conversation, **Cowork is delegation.** You point Claude at a folder, describe the outcome, and it works on your real files.

## The three things AI cannot do well

To beat AI, your final draft must improve on what it gives you in three dimensions:

### 1. Concision
AI is wordy even when asked to cut. After every AI draft, cut 30%. Then cut another 10%.

### 2. Accuracy
AI fabricates. Examples from class:
- Claude invented a journal-keeping habit Kramon never had
- ChatGPT regularly invents quotes, dates, and source attributions

**Rule:** verify every fact, every name, every number, every quote before sending.

### 3. Warmth and personality
The hardest thing for AI to replicate, and the most important. The PS that makes them smile. The line of dialogue that proves you were there. The detail that's too specific to have been generated.

## AI tells — scrub these

ChatGPT-flavored constructions that mark a draft as machine-written:

- *"It's not just X — it's Y."* (the most common tic)
- *"In today's [adjective] world"*
- *"Delve into"*
- *"Navigate the complexities of"*
- *"Tapestry of"*
- *"Robust solution"*
- *"Cutting-edge"*
- *"Game-changer"*
- Em-dashes used as a tic (one or two per page is fine — twelve is a tell)
- Tricolons everywhere ("X, Y, and Z" three times in three sentences)
- Symmetric three-paragraph structure with no asymmetry
- Hedge-then-commit: *"While there are many considerations, ultimately…"*
- **Choppy short-sentence stacks.** Four or five very short sentences in a row. Like this. It looks punchy. It isn't. It's the new AI tell. Mix in medium and long sentences so the prose has rhythm — short bursts work *because* of contrast with what surrounds them.
- **Em-dashes — the #1 AI tell in 2026.** Models love them. Humans use them sparingly. For email and memo: replace every em-dash with a comma, period, parenthesis, or colon. For long-form: one or two per page is fine; ten is a confession. Common rewrites: "X — Y" → "X. Y." or "X, Y" or "X (Y)" or "X: Y". Double-hyphens (--) are AI substitutes for em-dashes — same problem.

## The Cowork workflow

The high-leverage setup, from the *Claude Cowork* essay:

### 1. Point Claude at a real folder
Not snippets pasted into chat. Claude reads, edits, and creates files in your actual workspace.

### 2. Build three context files

Spend 20 minutes once. These do more for output quality than any prompt engineering.

**`about-me.md`** — who you are, what you do, what you're working on right now. Brief a new colleague, don't write a resume.

**`voice-and-style.md`** — how you write. Tone, sentence structure, phrases you like, phrases you'd never use. Paste 2–3 examples of your actual writing. Be specific about words to ban: *"never use 'delve,' 'leverage,' 'I hope this email finds you well,' or 'in today's fast-paced world.'"*

**`context.md`** (or topic-specific files) — what you're working on, who the players are, what's already been decided.

### 3. Set a global instruction
Settings → Cowork → Edit Global Instructions:
> *"Before every task, read everything in my context files."*

Now every session starts with Claude knowing how you think and write.

### 4. Describe outcomes, not steps
Stop saying *"first read this, then summarize, then turn it into bullets."* Say *"turn this article into a one-page briefing memo for my team, in my voice, formatted as bullets."* Claude figures out the steps. You stay focused on the destination.

### 5. Use Projects for recurring workflows
Dedicated workspaces with their own context: *"Networking outreach,"* *"Case write-ups,"* *"Job search,"* *"Weekly memos."* Each one gets smarter at its domain over time.

### 6. Connect tools (advanced)
Cowork plugs into Google Drive, Gmail, Calendar, and more. This is where it goes from *"Claude can edit my files"* to *"Claude can pull context from my calendar, draft a prep doc, and save it to the right folder."*

## When NOT to use AI

- **Gratitude notes.** Must be authentic. Handwritten beats typed.
- **Anything emotional or personal** where the recipient will sense the absence of you
- **Six-word summaries.** The compression has to come from your own decisions about what matters
- **Final pass before sending.** Always read aloud yourself

## The standing AI checklist

Run after every AI-assisted draft, before sending:

- [ ] Cut 30% of words
- [ ] Verified every name, number, date, quote
- [ ] Removed AI tells (`it's not just X`, `delve`, `tapestry`, etc.)
- [ ] Added at least one specific sensory detail only I would know
- [ ] Read aloud — does it sound like me?
- [ ] If a friend read this blind, would they know it's me?

If the answer to the last question is *"could be anyone"* — start over.
