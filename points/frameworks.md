# Frameworks

Eight frameworks taught in *Winning Writing*, in the order you'd reach for them.

## 1. BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

Military principle. The conclusion goes in the first sentence. Context, evidence, and qualification come after.

**Bad (organ music):** "Over the past quarter we evaluated several scenarios and after extensive analysis with the team we believe that…"
**BLUF:** "We need to lower Q3 sales forecast by 50%. Three reasons:"

Apply BLUF to: emails, memos, status updates, board reports, performance reviews, op-eds.

## 2. S.H.I.T. — Pitch yourself or your company

Rachel Konrad's framework for any narrative pitch.

- **Simple** — especially if your background is complex, simplify it
- **Heroic** — position yourself or the company as the protagonist; will they prevail? Stay tuned
- **Inevitable** — what you're pursuing is on the right side of history
- **Timely** — you're doing this *now* for a specific reason

Works for: LinkedIn bio, "tell me about yourself," investor decks, founding-story essays.

## 3. The 7-part business pitch

For investors, bosses, or journalists.

1. **What it does** — in plain English, in one sentence
2. **Differentiation** — what's already out there and why you're different
3. **Customer examples and endorsements** — specific names, specific numbers
4. **Market size and funding** — TAM, dollars raised, runway
5. **Superhero story** — why *you* are the one to do this
6. **News hook** — why this is timely
7. **Bigger trend** — connect to the wave (especially if you're small)

### The six-word test
Summarize your product in six words. If you can't, you don't know it well enough yet.

## 4. Mission-statement template

Four sentences:

1. *"This problem [X] is a dire threat, even if most people don't know it."*
2. *"My company is developing the most elegant solution: [how it works]."*
3. *"We might fail, but the mission is so important we must try."*
4. *"Call to action: [specific ask — invest, join, legislate, hire me]."*

## 5. Op-ed structure

The five questions to answer before you write a word:

1. **Who is your audience?**
2. **Which publication?**
3. **Why you?** What makes you uniquely qualified — were you in the room? Do you have lived experience?
4. **Why now?** What makes this timely?
5. **What's your solution?** Don't be a Debbie Downer. Provide answers, not just complaints.

Structure:
- Open with a **cinematic anecdote** — real person, vivid scene
- State a **clear thesis** with a fresh angle
- Use **specific examples** with real names and numbers
- **Refute the best counterargument** (not a strawman)
- Provide **actionable solutions**
- Connect to a **broader trend**

Target: **500–800 words.**

### What gets you published
- Interview real people in person or on Zoom — not just web research
- Co-author with a recognized expert (dramatically increases publication odds)
- Cinematic examples — real people as heroes
- Universality — the best pieces make readers say "that's happening everywhere"

## 6. The 10-rule cold-email framework

See [cold-email-rules.md](cold-email-rules.md). Summary:

1. Know something about them — exploit it
2. Begin with what they don't know — "tell me a secret about the future"
3. Subject line that cannot be ignored — personal, timely, unusual
4. Find a mutual contact and name them
5. "Like you" — specific, genuine, not self-diminishing
6. Tell a story that makes them feel something — multisensory scene
7. Pick a lane — one example, not your whole resume
8. Be confident but humble — "desirable confidence"
9. Short with a small, easy ask — under 200 words
10. Offer something in return — invite, don't request

## 7. Gratitude-note formula

The hardest assignment in Kramon's class.

Goal: make the recipient say *"tears streaming down my face"* and *"I'll print and save this."*

- **Be cinematic** — help them visualize a specific moment, with date and place
- **State the impact on your life**, using "even now" language ("Even now, when I'm stuck on a problem, I hear you say…")
- **Send to people who rarely get thanked** — admins, support staff, teachers, parents
- **Write when you feel emotional** about the memory
- **Handwritten** beats typed; physical mail beats email
- **Do not use AI.** This is the one piece of writing you must write yourself

## 8. The six-word life summary

The ultimate concision drill.

Hemingway's: *"For sale: baby shoes, never worn."*
Kramon's: *"Edited Pulitzer winners. Now teaching business leaders."*
Student examples:
- *"Immigrant's kid, chip on her shoulder."*
- *"Soldier first, engineer always, educator next."*

Rule: six words that tell a **story** — not six unrelated descriptors.
