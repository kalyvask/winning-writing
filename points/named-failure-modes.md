# Named failure modes

The 12-dimension rubric tells you *where* a cold email scored low. Naming the failure tells you *what to fix*. Use this catalog when grading or gating: when an email fails, name the specific mode in plain English.

The catalog has 14 modes across three categories:

- **Strategy failures** — about what you're asking
- **Personalization failures** — about the connection
- **Posture failures** — about how the sender comes across

Any one named failure should block a send. The point of naming is so the sender knows what to rewrite, not where they scored low.

---

## Strategy failures

### 1. Vague ask

The ask isn't binary. The recipient has to invent the response.

**Tells:** "Would love your thoughts." "Any feedback?" "Happy to chat anytime." "Would be great to find time."

**Fix:** make it yes/no answerable in 30 seconds. "Are you open to a 15-min call next Wednesday at 2pm?" not "would love to find time."

### 2. No "why now"

The email could have been sent a year ago, or next year. Nothing anchors it to a specific moment in time.

**Tells:** no news hook, no deadline, no recent thing the recipient said or did, no trigger event.

**Fix:** name the trigger. "I read your podcast yesterday." "Your portfolio just announced X." "The May 1 deadline is the reason I'm writing this week, not next month."

### 3. Reaching across hierarchy without a reason

Asking someone several levels above with no warm bridge, no offer they can't get from their own staff, and no thesis they haven't heard.

**Tells:** the sender is junior, the recipient is a CEO/partner/EVP, no mutual contact, no novel data, no thing they cannot get from a research team.

**Fix:** find the warm bridge OR a secret-about-the-future thesis OR an offer that's specifically yours (your data, your interview, your audience).

### 4. Stranger asking for a job

First contact asks for hire/intro/funding rather than building the relationship through value-first contact.

**Tells:** email 1 says "I'd love to be considered for a PM role" or "could you intro me to your founders" with no prior interaction, no shared context, no thing offered.

**Fix:** offer something first. The job ask is email 3, not email 1. Email 1 should be useful regardless of whether they reply.

### 5. Resume dump

Multiple lanes — "I've done X and also Y and also Z" — instead of one specific story that proves the relevant lane.

**Tells:** the email lists 3+ companies, 3+ projects, or "I'm interested in [list of 4 things]."

**Fix:** pick one lane. The right lane is the one that maps to the recipient's stated focus.

## Personalization failures

### 6. Unverifiable connection

The "like you" line claims a connection the recipient cannot independently verify.

**Tells:** "We both believe in AI." "I admire your work on X" (with no specific reference). "I share your passion for [vague value]." Generic shared values, no named person, no quoted moment.

**Fix:** the connection must be specific enough that the recipient can confirm it: a named person, a named event, a quote with attribution, a thing they actually did.

### 7. Recap opener

The first sentence tells the recipient something they already know about themselves.

**Tells:** "You've transformed industry after industry…" "As CEO of X you've built…" "Congrats on the recent funding!" "Your bio is impressive."

**Fix:** open with something they don't know — your thesis, your number, a contradiction in their public position, a scene they haven't seen.

### 8. Generic personalization

Recipient name + one fact swapped into a structure that would work for any of their peers.

**Litmus test:** swap the recipient's name for another well-known person in their domain. Does the email still mostly work? If yes, this is the failure.

**Fix:** find the angle that's specifically about THIS person, not just a person in their role. Distinctive personal details (hobbies, formative experiences, unusual anecdotes) outrank role-level facts.

## Posture failures

### 9. Credentials dump

Front-loads credentials ("I'm an MBA at Stanford, ex-Snowflake, ex-Amazon, currently at…") instead of showing competence through specifics.

**Tells:** the first paragraph is a CV summary; named institutions appear before any concrete work.

**Fix:** prove competence with one specific scene the recipient cares about. Credentials, if any, go at the end as evidence — not the lead.

### 10. Humble brag

False-modesty hedges that signal insecurity, not warmth.

**Tells:** "At a much smaller scale," "while not as accomplished as you," "though I'm sure I have much to learn from you," "like you, but at a vastly smaller scale."

**Fix:** delete. Confidence without arrogance. Adam Bryant's "desirable confidence."

### 11. Throat-clearing

Words spent before the email begins.

**Tells:** "I hope this email finds you well." "I'm reaching out because…" "My name is X and I'm a…" "Good morning." "Sorry to bother you."

**Fix:** delete the throat-clearing. Open with the BLUF (bottom line up front).

### 12. Fabricated specifics

A name, number, quote, or event the email presents as fact but the dossier doesn't substantiate.

**Tells:** "You said on your podcast that…" with no quote in the dossier. "Your portfolio company X is doing Y" with neither X nor Y named in research. "We met at the Sequoia event in March" with no record of it.

**Fix:** if you can't verify, don't write it. Better to say less than to fabricate. Flag for the sender to verify before sending.

### 13. AI-tell prose

Sentences a model wrote and a human wouldn't.

**Tells:** "It's not just X — it's Y." 3+ em-dashes used for emphasis. "Delve into," "tapestry of," "navigate the complexities," "in today's fast-paced world." 5+ short sentences in a row. Words like "robust solution," "cutting-edge," "game-changer."

**Fix:** rewrite the offending sentences in plain English. Read aloud — does it sound like a human typing or a model showing off?

### 14. Empty intensifiers

Words that add no information.

**Tells:** "Would love to." "Would be incredible." "Truly." "Genuinely." "Absolutely." "Really." "Very." Sentence-starters like "Importantly," "Notably," "Crucially."

**Fix:** delete or replace with substance. If "I'd love to chat" loses meaning when you cut "love," the sentence wasn't carrying any.

---

## How to use this catalog

When grading: name the specific failure mode rather than just citing the rubric dimension. "FAIL: vague ask" is more actionable than "Ask scored 4/10."

When gating: any one named mode is a hard fail. Borderline → fail. The cost of blocking a marginal email is one rewrite cycle. The cost of shipping a templated email is the recipient's read of the sender forever.

When fixing: rewrite the specific line that triggered the mode, not the whole email. Most failures are local, not structural.
