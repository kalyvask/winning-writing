# Kramon master reference

The complete distillation of Glenn Kramon's *Winning Writing* (GSBGEN 352.1), Stanford GSB. Pulled from all four sessions, including guest lectures by Rachel Konrad, Heidi Roizen, Katie Kingsbury (NYT Opinion), and Danny Hertzberg (Base10).

> *"People will forget what you said. People will forget what you did. But people will never forget how you made them feel."* — Maya Angelou

Before every piece: **how will this make them feel?**

---

## I. Foundational rules

1. **Know your main point** before you start
2. **Know your audience** — write *for* them, not *at* them
3. **Bottom line up front** (BLUF)
4. **Start with what they don't know** — not what they already know
5. **Be shorter** — always
6. **Vary sentence length** — creates rhythm
7. **Write as you'd speak** to family or friends
8. **Say what you like AND what you would like** — never only what you don't like
9. **Add warmth and humor** — a PS that makes them smile is powerful
10. **Assume any email could end up on the front page of the NYT**
11. **Use bold, bullets, white space** — make it scannable
12. **Eliminate the organ music** — don't build up, just get there

## II. Tone and voice

- **On your toes, not on your heels** — proactive, forward-leaning
- **Competence AND warmth** in a single sentence
- **Confident but humble** — avoid the GSB arrogance stereotype
- **Tell stories, not resume bullets** — 12× more memorable than statistics alone
- **Use "like you" comparisons** — instant connection

## III. Banned words and jargon

See [banned-jargon.md](banned-jargon.md). Headline offenders:

- `currently` (class-wide ban)
- `synergy`, `leverage`, `align`, `drive`, `strategize`, `empower`, `enable`
- `impact` → say **harm** or **benefits**
- `utilize` → **use**
- `irregardless` → **regardless**
- `at the intersection of [X, Y, Z]`
- *"I hope this email finds you well"*
- *"In today's fast-paced world"*

## IV. Grammar and mechanics

- **Who vs. whom** — whom for objects
- **Which vs. that** — that for essential clauses
- **Affect vs. effect**
- **Its vs. it's**
- **Adverse vs. averse**
- **Em dash (—)** for emphasis and breaks; **hyphen (-)** only for compound words
- **Formal address in cold emails:** *"Dear Mr./Ms. [Name]:"* — colon, not comma
- **Adverbs ending in -ly** are usually unnecessary; cut them

## V. Subject lines and headlines

- **Personal, specific, unusual** — never spam-like
- Op-ed headlines should make a **bold, arguable claim** — not a neutral description

Examples from class:
- *"Olympians earn the IOC billions. Guess who the IOC almost never pays."*
- *"Option trading is rigged against average investors."*
- *"The hottest restaurants should be the ones that care about their workers."*
- *"Why women should coach boys' sports."*

## VI. Cold email — see [cold-email-rules.md](cold-email-rules.md)

Make yourself **"one in a million, not one of a million."** Answer two questions: **Why you? Why now?**

## VII. Op-eds

### Five questions to answer first
1. Who is your audience?
2. Which publication?
3. Why you? (Were you in the room? Lived experience?)
4. Why now?
5. What's your solution? (Don't be a Debbie Downer)

### Structure
- 500–800 words
- Cinematic anecdote opener
- Clear thesis with a fresh angle
- Specific examples — real names, real numbers
- Refute the best counterargument
- Actionable solutions
- Connect to broader trend

### What gets published
- **Interview real people** — in person or Zoom, not just web
- **Cinematic examples** — real people as heroes
- **Why-you is essential** — the Stanford Law student got published because she was *in the room*
- **Co-author with a recognized expert** — dramatically increases publication odds
- **Timeliness** — editors ask "why run this now?"
- **Universality** — the best pieces make readers say "that's happening everywhere"

### Katie Kingsbury (NYT Opinion)
- NYT Opinion grew from 50 → 225+ staff since 2017
- They more often **commission** pieces than accept unsolicited
- **Resubmit if declined** — she encourages it
- **Jargon removal** is a top priority — write for a general audience
- **Storytelling is crucial** — personal narratives, character profiles
- Anonymous bylines are extremely rare (one in 10 years)
- Growing concern: sources pulling pieces due to fear of reprisal

## VIII. Business pitch

### The 7-part pitch
1. What it does — plain English, one sentence
2. Differentiation
3. Customer examples and endorsements
4. Market size and funding
5. Superhero story — why you
6. News hook — why now
7. Bigger trend

### The six-word test
Summarize your product in six words. If you can't, you don't know it well enough.

### S.H.I.T. framework — Simple, Heroic, Inevitable, Timely

### Mission-statement template
1. *"This problem is a dire threat, even if most people don't know it."*
2. *"My company is developing the most elegant solution: [how it works]."*
3. *"We might fail, but the mission is so important we must try."*
4. *"Call to action: [specific ask]."*

## IX. Memo and executive writing

- BLUF → context → specific ask
- **Avoid complaints** — propose solutions
- Include **next steps** and volunteer for implementation
- Bold, bullets, white space
- **Add warmth and personality** — you're writing to a human
- Humor is allowed, even encouraged (the *"I'm hungry, I'm sleepy"* memo)
- Bad example: the Stanford Provost's welcome email — too long, told people what they already knew, buried the actual news

## X. Gratitude notes

Kramon's favorite assignment.

- **Goal:** make the recipient say *"tears streaming down my face"* and *"I'll print and save this"*
- **Be cinematic** — visualize specific moments
- **Specific impact** using "even now" language
- **Send to people who rarely get thanks** — admins, support staff, teachers
- **Write when you feel emotional**
- **Handwritten** delivery beats typed
- **Do NOT use AI** — must be authentic and personal

## XI. AI writing — see [ai-writing-rules.md](ai-writing-rules.md)

> *"You want to be one in a million. AI by definition makes you one of a million."*

- AI is a tool, **not a ghostwriter** — keep your hands on the keyboard
- Beat AI by improving on what it gives you in three ways:
  1. **Be more concise** — AI is wordy even when asked to cut
  2. **Verify accuracy** — AI fabricates (Claude invented a journal-keeping habit for Kramon that didn't exist)
  3. **Add warmth and personality** — the hardest thing for AI to replicate
- Aim to be a **"centaur"** — human + AI for best results
- Build a **personal context vault**: interests, controversial thoughts, essays
- AI tells: *"It's not just X — it's Y"*, em-dash overuse, tricolons everywhere

## XII. LinkedIn profile

- The **most important paragraph** in the first half of your career
- Change it strategically for specific opportunities
- Include a **personal thesis** on why you're uniquely valuable
- Avoid generic buzzwords
- Use it as a platform to showcase writing samples

## XIII. Concision drills

- **Six-word life summary** — tell a story in six words
- **Rewrite this in less than 100 words** — the standard exercise

## XIV. Responding to tough questions

- **Write out your answers first** — helps you think clearly and stay on message
- Applies to journalists, employees, boards — any high-stakes audience
- Never just say what you don't like — always frame constructively

## XV. Kramon's personal rules for the class

1. Say what you like AND what you would like — never only what you don't like
2. People come in two types: givers and takers — be a giver
3. Humor is non-negotiable — if you think there's too much humor, drop the course
4. Constructiveness is mandatory — criticism without solutions is not welcome
5. Show your best work — only show work you're proud of; never shame students publicly
