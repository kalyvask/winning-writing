# Concision drill

Source: `points/core-rules.md` (rule 5: be shorter), `points/banned-jargon.md` (wordy phrases), `points/frameworks.md` (six-word summary).

## The principle

> *"Be shorter. Always. Cut without losing substance."* — Kramon

Most drafts can lose 30% with no loss of meaning. Many can lose 50%. The exercise of forced compression is what reveals which sentences were actually carrying weight.

## Three drill levels

### Level 1 — Cut 30%
Standard pass. Aim: 30% fewer words, same meaning, same voice.

Tactics:
- Remove every adverb ending in -ly that isn't doing real work
- Replace wordy phrases (see [banned-jargon.md](../../points/banned-jargon.md))
- Combine sentences that share a subject
- Cut hedges: *"I think,"* *"perhaps,"* *"it might be the case that"*
- Cut throat-clearing: *"In this piece I'll argue that…"* — just argue it
- Replace passive with active

### Level 2 — Hit a target word count
User gives a number (200 for a cold email, 500 for an op-ed, 100 for a memo). Iterate until you hit it.

Process:
1. Cut to 1.5× the target — easy passes only
2. Cut to 1.2× the target — harder; remove subordinate clauses
3. Cut to 1.0× the target — surgical; every word earns its place
4. Cut to 0.9× the target — to leave room for one final addition

### Level 3 — Six-word summary
The ultimate compression. One thing, six words, tells a story.

Run the user through:
1. What's the single most important thing about this?
2. Who is the protagonist?
3. What changed?
4. Try a draft. Score it.
5. Iterate.

Examples that work:
- *"For sale: baby shoes, never worn."* (Hemingway)
- *"Edited Pulitzer winners. Now teaching business leaders."* (Kramon)
- *"Immigrant's kid, chip on her shoulder."* (student)
- *"Soldier first, engineer always, educator next."* (student)

The rule: six words that tell a **story** — not six unrelated descriptors.

## What not to cut

Concision is not amputation. **Keep:**
- Specific sensory details (a date, a place, a sound, an image) — these are what make writing memorable
- "Like you" lines and personal hooks
- The one moment of warmth or humor
- Names and numbers

**Cut first:**
- Throat-clearing
- Hedges
- Adverbs
- Synonyms used for variety ("she said… she explained… she argued…")
- Unnecessary qualifiers
- Background that the reader can infer

## The two-pass method

1. **First pass** — read aloud. Cross out any sentence you stumble over. Most "stumbles" are sentences that aren't earning their place.
2. **Second pass** — read again. For each surviving sentence, ask: *what changes if I delete this?* If nothing, delete.

## Output format

When you finish a concision drill, hand back:

1. **The cut version** at the target length
2. **A diff summary** — what was removed, in three categories: (a) jargon, (b) throat-clearing/hedges, (c) substantive cuts (with one-line justification each)
3. **Word count** — original → final
4. **One flag** if there's a sentence the user might want to put back, with the tradeoff explained

## When the user resists cutting

Common pushback: *"but that detail is important."*

Response: *"Then it should earn its place. Show me which other sentence we can cut to keep it. We can't keep both."*

Concision is zero-sum.
